# Trade-tongue: language reference

The common language of The Stranded. This file expands the starter vocabulary into a more developed (not complete) language with working grammar, vocabulary by domain, and example sentences. The main design doc holds the short version; this is the full reference.

## What trade-tongue is

The common language formed during the resettlement era. It has three layers:

- **Sehari grammar and structure** underneath. The Sehari were here longest; their language is the substrate.
- **Native Sehari roots** for planet-tied concepts: crystals, energy, souls, stone, ruins, kinship, the deep things.
- **English-distorted vocabulary** for everyday life: food, trade, tools, common actions, numbers. This is the practical layer, tied to the Mi'naa, who were the bulk of the resettlement population and who built the writing system.
- **Iskari structural particles** that hold sentences together: subject and object markers, tense, prepositions. The Iskari were translators by design, so their contribution is grammatical clarity. Trade-tongue avoids ambiguity even where context would resolve it.

**Register matters.** A speaker leaning on Sehari roots (a Bone-singer, a Keeper, an old Iskari) sounds deep and archaic. A speaker leaning on English-distorted words (a Mi'naa trader, a scavenger) sounds plain and modern. Same language, different registers.

## Pronunciation

Soft and flowing. Vowels are open and pure (a as in "father," e as in "bet," i as in "machine," o as in "go," u as in "flute"). Stress usually falls on the first syllable of a root. Apostrophes (as in Tel'sharin, Mi'naa) mark a slight glottal pause between two distinct sounds. Hyphens in compounds are written but lightly spoken, almost a single word.

## Grammar

### Word order

Basic order is subject, verb, object: **mi te hach kin** (I have crystal). The subject takes the marker **te**, the object takes the marker **ne** when clarity is needed.

- **mi te hach kin-rava ne.** — I have crystal pieces.

Prepositions come before their noun. Adjectives come after their noun.

- **lo vesh** — toward the ruin
- **vesh mel** — old ruin (ruin-old)

### Subject and object markers

- **te** marks the subject (the actor).
- **ne** marks the object (the thing acted on). Often dropped in short clauses or when the object is obvious, but used in formal or ambiguous sentences. The Iskari influence keeps it present more often than a casual speaker would bother with.

### Pronouns

| | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | **mi** (I, me) | **wi** (we, us) |
| 2nd | **yu** (you) | **yul** (you all) |
| 3rd | **ta** (he/she/they, no gender) | **tai** (they) |

Trade-tongue does not mark gender in pronouns. This fits a world where the Iskari have no gender and gendered language is generally light. **ta** covers any single person of any gender or none.

### Possession

The suffix **-na** marks possession, attached to the possessor.

- **mi-na fren** — my friend
- **yu-na pak** — your pack
- **ta-na kin** — their crystal
- **shari-na vish** — the kin's soul / the soul of the people

(Note: **na** standing before a verb is negation; **-na** suffixed to a noun is possession. Position disambiguates.)

### Tense

Tense is marked by a particle before the verb. Present is unmarked.

- Present: **mi te wak** — I walk.
- Past: **ru** before the verb. **mi te ru wak** — I walked.
- Future: **fa** before the verb. **mi te fa wak** — I will walk.
- Continuous/stative: **-lai** suffixed to the verb. **mi te wak-lai** — I am walking. **kin shin-lai** — the crystal is glowing.

### Negation

**na** before the verb (after any tense particle).

- **mi te na wak** — I do not walk.
- **mi te ru na wak** — I did not walk.
- **ta te fa na kom** — they will not come.

### Questions

Two ways.

Yes/no questions take the particle **ke** at the end:
- **yu te hach kin ke?** — Do you have crystal?

Content questions use interrogatives at the front:
- **hwat** (what), **hu** (who), **hwer** (where), **hwen** (when), **hwai** (why), **hau** (how)
- **hwer yu te go?** — Where are you going?
- **hwai tai te hunt wi?** — Why do they hunt us?

### Plurals

Often unmarked; number comes from context. For emphasis or clarity, the suffix **-en**:

- **vesh** — ruin / ruins (context decides)
- **vesh-en** — ruins (explicitly plural)

**shari** is inherently plural (the kin, the people); its singular is **shen** (a person). The **-ari** suffix means "people of," used for peoples: Sehari, Iskari.

### Comparatives

Particles after the adjective:

- **mor** (more), **mos** (most), **les** (less), and **dan** (than)
- **mel mor** — older (more old)
- **mel mos** — oldest
- **vesh mel mor dan bul nu** — a ruin older than a new building

### Conjunctions and connectors

- **enu** — and, with
- **o** — or
- **bat** — but
- **so** — so, therefore
- **dai** — when, while (joins clauses in time)
- **ef** — if
- **dan** — than
- **dat** — that (introduces a reported clause: "said that...")

### Prepositions

- **lo** — toward, into
- **fro** — from
- **sou** — under, beneath
- **ov** — over, above, of
- **in** — in, inside
- **aut** — out, outside
- **bai** — by, near, beside
- **tru** — through
- **at** — at (place or time)

### Derivation (making new words)

- **-er** — agent, one who does. **trad** (trade) to **trad-er** (trader); **wak** (walk) to **wak-er** (walker); **ward** (guard) to **ward-er** (a guard). Distinct from **-ari** ("people of," for whole peoples).
- **-sa** — material, substance of. **salt-sa** (salt as a substance), **mura-sa** (stone material).
- **-rava** — piece, fragment of. **kin-rava** (crystal pieces).
- **-lai** — stative (see tense).
- **-na** — possessive (see possession).
- **-en** — plural (see plurals).

Compounds are formed freely by joining roots with a hyphen, often a verb plus a noun: **hach-mem** (hold-memory = to remember), **ded-waka** (dead-walker, the folk word for the dispatch).

## Numbers

The Mi'naa designed the number system: base ten, regular, practical. The written notation handles large numbers cleanly (the script is logical by design).

| Number | Word | Number | Word |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **wun** | 6 | **sis** |
| 2 | **tu** | 7 | **sev** |
| 3 | **thri** | 8 | **eit** |
| 4 | **for** | 9 | **nain** |
| 5 | **faiv** | 10 | **ten** |

Teens are **ten** plus the digit: **ten-wun** (11), **ten-tu** (12). Tens are the digit plus **ten**: **tu-ten** (20), **thri-ten** (30). Compounds stack: **tu-ten-faiv** (25), **for-ten-thri** (43).

Larger: **hundu** (100), **thauz** (1000). **tu-hundu** (200), **faiv-hundu-eit-ten** (580).

## Time and the world's events

- **dey** — day, the light period
- **nit** — night, the dark period
- **tern** — one full day-night cycle (a "turn")
- **now** — now
- **past** — the past, before
- **shun** — soon
- **aza-brek** — the great forgetting, the catastrophe (memory-break)
- **ripl** — a ripple, one of the recurring lesser forgettings

## Example sentences

### Basic

**Shari te wak lo vesh mel.**
The people walk towards the old ruins.

**Mi te hach salt ne, lo trad kin-rava.**
I have salt to trade for crystal pieces.

**Gud dey. Yu te hach mit enu brot ke?**
Good day. Do you have meat and bread?

### Intermediate

**Trad-er te ru sai dat band-shen te ru stol kin-rava fro bul at nit.**
The trader said that the scavenger stole crystal pieces from the building at night.

**Mi te na want fait, bat mi te fa ward mi-na fren-en.**
I do not want to fight, but I will defend my friends.

**Nara mel te hach-mem mor, dan shari nu te nou.**
The old water remembers more than the young kin know.

**Hwer yu te go, enu hwai yu te kari ded-waka-na tul?**
Where are you going, and why are you carrying a dispatch's tool?

### Advanced

**Ef yu te vael sael sou vesh mel, yu te fa lis shari hu ru kom past.**
If you channel the energy beneath the old ruins, you will hear the kin who came before.

**Dai ripl te kom, Iskari te seha enu slip; tai te wak-lai na, til hath te kom nu.**
When a ripple comes, the Iskari grow still and sleep; they do not wake until the light comes again.
(*til* = until, English-derived; introduced here.)

**Ded-waka te sta hunt isk-shari, bat ta te na nou hwai; ta-na aza te brek long past.**
The dispatch still hunt the stone-kin, but they do not know why; their memory broke long ago.

**Sou mura, isk-shari-na vish-en te seha; sael te shael tai lo aza ov thal.**
Beneath the stone, the souls of the enduring ones wait; the energy weaves them into the deep memory of the land.
(Bone-singer register, Sehari-heavy.)

**Ef wi te brek di anomali, ol mesaj te fa go aut at wun taim, enu di aut-shari te fa nou hwat ru hapen.**
If we break the anomaly, all the messages will go out at once, and the outsiders will learn what happened.
(*anomali*, *mesaj*, *ol* (all), *taim* (time), *hapen* (happen), *aut-shari* (outside-people, outsiders) introduced here; the register tilts English-heavy because the speaker is talking about practical, modern, off-world matters.)

## Proposed etymologies (for the open question on Sehari roots)

The main design doc marks "what Isk and Seh mean" as OPEN. Proposals, consistent with the vocabulary above:

- **Seh** — from **seha**, stillness/waiting/the predator's quiet. **Sehari** = "people of stillness," or "the still ones." Fits their watchful, grounded, predator-calm nature.
- **Isk** — proposed to mean "enduring, lasting, that which remains." **Iskari** = "the enduring people," "those who remain." The Sehari named them after the catastrophe, seeing stone-skinned beings who did not age and stood still as statues through the ripples. Poignant, given the Iskari are the last carriers of the vanished Atlantians.

Both are proposals, not yet locked. They would make **Sehari** and **Iskari** rhyme in meaning as well as sound: the still ones and the enduring ones.

## Notes on use at the table

A full conlang is not needed to run the game. The practical use is flavour: drop trade-tongue words into roleplay for texture. Greetings (**gud dey**), common items (**kin**, **salt**, **brot**), place and people names, and a few resonant concepts (**sael**, **vish**, **aza**, **seha**) carry most of the weight. The grammar and the advanced sentences exist for when you want written inscriptions, ritual speech, or a Keeper's archive to feel real.
